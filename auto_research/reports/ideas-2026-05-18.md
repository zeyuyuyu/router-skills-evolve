## Top 3 Relevant Papers

### 1. `arxiv:2602.06717` — F-GRPO: Don't Let Your Policy Learn the Obvious and Forget the Rare
*Feb 2026*

Identifies a frequency bias in group-based policy optimization: with small group sizes
(G=4), gradient updates over-weight common-correct trajectories and under-weight
rare-correct trajectories on harder problems. The paper derives the super-linear
relationship between success rate and probability of missing rare-correct modes, and
proposes a Focal-loss-inspired difficulty-aware advantage scaling:

```
advantage_scaled = advantage × (1 - success_rate)^gamma
```

With gamma=2, this down-weights the ~20 easy tasks (high success rate) by 4–9× relative
to the ~27 tasks with mixed outcomes (2/4 pass), giving the hard segment of the training
distribution proportionally more gradient signal. No extra parameters, no extra compute.

**Why relevant today:** Our best case has ~53/100 eval tasks with zero successful rollouts
and ~20/100 with near-perfect rollouts — exactly the distribution F-GRPO targets. The
focal scaling could convert those ~27 mixed tasks from weak to strong gradient sources
and, if the all-fail tasks happen to have any successful rollout at all (even 1/4), those
get amplified most. This is the highest-priority experiment in today's batch.

---

### 2. `arxiv:2503.14476` — DAPO: An Open-Source LLM Reinforcement Learning System at Scale
*ByteDance Seed, March 2025*

DAPO introduces four orthogonal improvements to GRPO: (1) **Dynamic sampling** — discard
groups where all rollouts share the same reward (all pass or all fail) and resample until
every group has non-zero reward variance; (2) **Clip-Higher** — asymmetric importance
sampling clipping (higher upper clip than lower) to preserve exploration entropy;
(3) **Token-level policy gradient loss** instead of sample-level, stabilizing training
for variable-length code outputs; (4) Remove KL penalty (our baseline already does this).
Together, DAPO achieves 50pts on AIME 2024 with Qwen2.5-32B using 50% fewer training
steps than DeepSeek-R1-Zero on the same base.

**Why relevant today:** Dynamic sampling + token-level loss are implementation-only changes
with no hyperparameter tuning required. Dynamic sampling directly eliminates the zero-gradient
group problem, which our history suggests affects ~73% of training groups. Token-level loss
provides more granular gradient signal for code outputs where correct solutions have
variable length. These two DAPO techniques together form a clean ablation point.

---

### 3. `arxiv:2510.19807` — Scaf-GRPO: Scaffolded GRPO for Enhancing LLM Reasoning
*Oct 2025, revised Feb 2026*

Scaf-GRPO monitors per-task rolling success rate and, when a task stagnates (zero reward
across N consecutive gradient steps), injects tiered pedagogical hints into the prompt —
first abstract ("identify the algorithmic pattern"), then concrete ("use a dictionary to
track frequencies"). The model processes hint + problem under a single policy, preserving
exploration flexibility while providing a "signpost" rather than a "railroad." On AIME24
with Qwen2.5-Math-7B, Scaf-GRPO achieves a **44.3% relative improvement** over vanilla
GRPO by recovering learning signal on tasks the base GRPO treated as permanently invisible.

**Why relevant today:** Our 53/100 all-fail eval tasks represent a permanent zero-gradient
black hole in our training set. Scaf-GRPO's stagnation detection + hint injection
mechanism is designed precisely for this case: tasks where the model cannot find a valid
rollout without guidance. A stagnation window of 3 steps (threshold before hint injection)
and two hint levels (abstract → concrete) give the model a recovery path without requiring
pre-computed difficulty ratings.

**Rationale:** F-GRPO (arxiv:2602.06717) introduces Focal-loss-inspired difficulty-aware
advantage scaling: `advantage_scaled = advantage × (1 - success_rate)^gamma`. With
gamma=2, groups where the model already solves most rollouts are down-weighted, while
the mixed-outcome groups (hardest tasks with 1–3/4 successes) receive proportionally
more gradient weight. Our training distribution is heavily skewed: ~20/100 trivially
easy (all-pass, near-zero advantage), ~53/100 completely hard (all-fail, zero advantage),
and only ~27/100 in the mixed regime where GRPO actually learns. Focal scaling amplifies
the signal from these 27 informative groups by up to 4× and can reclaim gradient signal
from any hard task that manages even 1 successful rollout. All other params identical to
the best case. The focal advantage scale requires no extra parameters and negligible
compute overhead (~1% for computing per-task success rate statistics). This is the
**highest-priority** 2026-05-18 experiment — it targets the root cause of why our
best-case gain is only +2pts.

### EXP-015 · `exp_2026_05_18_002_dapo_dynamic_sampling_15b` · Priority 8
**Kind:** `grpo_continual`
**Model:** Qwen2.5-Coder-1.5B-Instruct
**Spec:** 200 MBPP tasks, 4 rollouts/prompt, 1 epoch, lr=5e-6, LoRA r=16, qwen-chat,
binary reward, `dynamic_sampling=True`, `loss_level="token"`, eval@100

**Rationale:** DAPO (arxiv:2503.14476) demonstrates that dynamic sampling — discarding
groups where all rollouts have identical reward and replacing them from the remaining
prompt pool — eliminates zero-gradient groups and produces substantially cleaner policy
updates. Our analysis suggests ~73% of training groups are uninformative under standard
GRPO: ~53% all-fail (zero reward) and ~20% all-pass (near-zero advantage). Dynamic
sampling ensures that each gradient step uses only groups with genuine reward signal.
Combined with token-level policy gradient loss (which provides more stable gradients
for variable-length code outputs), these two DAPO techniques require no hyperparameter
changes and add only ~10% sampling overhead. DAPO achieved 50/90 on AIME2024 with
Qwen2.5-32B; the dynamic sampling mechanism scales down to our 1.5B setting since
it is task-distribution-agnostic. Complementary to EXP-014 (F-GRPO): F-GRPO
re-weights existing groups while DAPO removes uninformative ones — different solutions
to the same root cause.

**Estimated GPU time:** ~95 minutes

**Runner changes required:** Add `dynamic_sampling` and `loss_level` fields to
`grpo_continual` kind in `runner.py`.

### EXP-016 · `exp_2026_05_18_003_scafgrpo_hint_injection_15b` · Priority 7
**Kind:** `grpo_continual`
**Model:** Qwen2.5-Coder-1.5B-Instruct
**Spec:** 200 MBPP tasks, 4 rollouts/prompt, 1 epoch, lr=5e-6, LoRA r=16, qwen-chat,
binary reward, `scaffold_stagnation_window=3`, `scaffold_hint_levels=["abstract","concrete"]`,
max_new_tokens=256, eval@100

**Rationale:** Scaf-GRPO (arxiv:2510.19807) targets the "learning cliff" — tasks where
the model consistently fails all rollouts across many gradient steps, rendering them
invisible to the policy gradient. The framework injects tiered in-prompt hints only
after stagnation is detected (3+ consecutive all-fail steps per task), avoiding hint
reliance for tasks the model can solve independently. For our 1.5B MBPP case, the ~53
all-fail tasks are permanently zero-gradient without intervention. Scaf-GRPO's abstract
hint ("identify the algorithmic pattern in this problem") followed by a concrete hint
("use a hash map to track the frequency of each element") provides a recovery path
for at least a subset of these tasks. On AIME24, Scaf-GRPO gave a 44.3% relative
gain over vanilla GRPO by recovering precisely this class of "permanently failed" tasks.
The hint injection is a minimal code change: add a per-task success tracker and a
conditional prompt extension. max_new_tokens increased to 256 to accommodate the
augmented prompt.

**Estimated GPU time:** ~110 minutes

**Runner changes required:** Add `scaffold_stagnation_window` and `scaffold_hint_levels`
fields to `grpo_continual` kind; implement per-task rolling success tracker and hint
injection logic in the training loop.

### EXP-017 · `exp_2026_05_18_004_frpo_2epoch_15b_200x4` · Priority 6
**Kind:** `grpo_continual`
**Model:** Qwen2.5-Coder-1.5B-Instruct
**Spec:** 200 MBPP tasks, 4 rollouts/prompt, **epochs=2**, lr=5e-6, LoRA r=16, qwen-chat,
binary reward, `frpo_mode=True`, eval@100

**Rationale:** Our entire history uses epochs=1. Multi-epoch training would double the
learning signal from the same 200 tasks, but the mechanistic analysis
(arxiv:2601.18699) shows that catastrophic forgetting accelerates in epochs 3–5 for
standard fine-tuning. FRPO (arxiv:2602.08813) directly addresses this: it modifies
GRPO with a max-min formulation — maximise the minimum reward over a KL-bounded
neighbourhood of policies reachable by downstream adaptation. The dual form yields
an entropic-risk objective that up-weights low-reward rollouts and penalises
high-variance policies. Crucially, FRPO adds no extra computation and is shown to
preserve accuracy under subsequent fine-tuning, which is mechanistically equivalent
to preventing within-run epoch-to-epoch forgetting. Running 2 epochs under FRPO
tests whether: (a) epoch 2 provides additional improvement over epoch 1 (the best
case), or (b) the 2nd epoch with FRPO degrades (confirming that even variance-aware
protection is insufficient for 2-epoch GRPO on 1.5B). Either outcome is highly
informative. If FRPO + 2 epochs beats standard GRPO + 1 epoch (+>2pts), multi-epoch
training becomes a zero-cost path to stronger results.

**Estimated GPU time:** ~165 minutes (2× training steps; still within 4h A800 budget)

**Runner changes required:** Add `frpo_mode` field to `grpo_continual` kind; implement
entropic-risk objective (penalise advantage variance within each group) in the loss
function.
