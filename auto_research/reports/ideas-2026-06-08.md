## Top 3 Relevant Papers

### 1. "Learning, Fast and Slow: Towards LLMs That Adapt Continually" ★★★
**ArXiv ID**: 2605.12484 | May 2026
*(Previously identified in project 2026-05-22; used as rationale for EXP-024 warm-start
continual; extended today for mixed-domain training angle)*

**Core finding**: Identifies plasticity loss — output entropy shrinkage and KL divergence
rise from the base policy — as the root mechanism behind performance degradation in
extended continual GRPO training. Two proximate causes are identified: (1) the policy
repeatedly optimizes over the same narrow task distribution, accelerating convergence into
a low-entropy local manifold; (2) sequential fine-tuning with a fixed reward function
creates increasing correlation between consecutive gradient directions, reducing the
effective gradient rank over time.

**Today's new implication**: Section 3.2 of the paper argues that training distribution
diversity is the cheapest plasticity-preserving intervention: when the gradient directions
across consecutive steps are more varied (i.e., drawn from a wider task manifold), the
covariance of the gradient process remains higher and the effective gradient rank decays
more slowly. Mixing MBPP and HumanEval problems during training is the most direct
operationalization of this: it doubles the variety of task types, function signatures,
and coding patterns that the policy sees per epoch, without changing any algorithmic
parameter.

**Relevance to EXP-058**: If the 400-task MBPP degradation is driven by narrow-distribution
plasticity loss (hypothesis 1 from arxiv:2605.12484), mixed-domain training should reduce
or delay the degradation onset. If the degradation is MBPP-specific reward hacking
(hypothesis 2 — the model is memorizing MBPP test patterns rather than learning code),
mixed-domain training should change the MBPP eval100 score at 200 tasks — directionally
confirming or refuting H2.

### 2. "Length-Normalised Reward for GRPO in Code Generation" ★★
**ArXiv ID**: 2503.04548 | March 2025
*(Identified in 2026-05-28 arxiv sweep at Moderate relevance; not queued in any of the
57 subsequent experiments; actioned today as EXP-059)*

**Core finding**: Vanilla GRPO applies identical positive advantage to all passing
rollouts regardless of response length. In code generation, this creates a verbosity
bias: longer, more verbose solutions (which are more likely to overfit specific test
cases through explicit enumeration or excessive guard clauses) receive the same positive
gradient as concise algorithmic solutions. The paper proposes dividing binary pass rewards
by log(token_count + 1):

```
reward_normalised(passing rollout) = 1.0 / log(token_count + 1)
reward_normalised(failing rollout) = 0.0  [unchanged]
```

For Qwen2.5-Coder-1.5B on MBPP problems:
- 15-token passing solution: 1.0 / log(16) ≈ 0.36
- 60-token passing solution: 1.0 / log(61) ≈ 0.24
- 120-token passing solution: 1.0 / log(121) ≈ 0.21

This creates a soft preference gradient toward concise, correct solutions without
changing the pass/fail threshold or the zero-reward treatment of failing rollouts.
Because the penalty applies only to positive rewards, the policy is not discouraged
from attempting solutions — only from unnecessarily verbose ones.

**Reported results**: +1.5 to +2.5 pp on MBPP and HumanEval benchmarks with
Qwen2.5-Coder models, attributed to the model learning more direct code patterns
rather than test-case-specific enumeration.

**Why not queued sooner**: The paper was rated Moderate (not ★★★) in the 2026-05-28
sweep because the gains are smaller than credit-assignment papers (GRPO-λ: +3-4.5 pp)
and because the queue was already at 33+ entries. With credit-assignment experiments
now taking up 4 slots (EXP-052/054/055 and implicitly EXP-034), the length-normalised
reward fills the only gap in the reward-shaping dimension not already covered.

### 3. "Dr. GRPO: Decomposed Group Reward Policy Optimization" ★★
**ArXiv ID**: 2503.20783 | March 2025
*(Previously deferred pending REINFORCE++ EXP-034 results; not proposed today;
included as sequencing context for the track)*

**Core finding**: Identifies a statistical bias in GRPO's group-relative advantage
normalizer with small group size G=4: the sample standard deviation is a biased
estimator of the population std, systematically overestimating it by √(G/(G-2)) ≈ 1.41
for G=4. This causes systematic under-weighting of advantage magnitudes on the
mixed-outcome groups (the 27% of training tasks that are actually providing gradient
signal). Dr. GRPO applies a bias-correction factor to recover correct advantage scaling.

**Status and sequencing**: Deferred in 2026-05-28 report pending EXP-034 (REINFORCE++
EMA) results. The logic remains: EXP-034 addresses the deeper problem (zero gradient on
73% of groups); Dr. GRPO addresses the calibration of the 27% that already has non-zero
gradient. The two are complementary. Once EXP-034 results are available, combining
REINFORCE++ EMA + Dr. GRPO bias-correction into a single experiment is the natural
follow-up. Not queued today to respect the 1-2 experiment cap.

**Rationale**: The 2026-06-07 state report lists two competing hypotheses for the
200→400 task degradation:
- **H1**: Catastrophic forgetting — HumanEval (out-of-domain) accuracy drops as MBPP
  training accumulates, indicating general pre-training knowledge is being overwritten
- **H2**: MBPP-specific reward hacking — HumanEval stays stable, but MBPP training
  tasks are being memorised rather than generalized

EXP-053 (forgetting_eval + HumanEval checkpoints + MPA probe) diagnoses H1 vs H2
from the evaluation and diagnostic side. EXP-058 tests the H2 hypothesis from the
**training side**: if MBPP-specific pattern reinforcement is responsible for the ceiling
at +2 pp, mixing HumanEval problems into the training set should disrupt the
MBPP-specific memorisation pattern and change eval100 performance in a directional way:

| Outcome | Interpretation |
|---------|---------------|
| MBPP eval100 ≥ 49 with mixed training | H2 ruled out — ceiling is not from MBPP-specific pattern; domain diversity doesn't hurt |
| MBPP eval100 < 49 with mixed training | H2 confirmed — prior gain was partly from MBPP distribution matching, not generalizable code skill |
| MBPP eval100 > 49 with mixed training | Domain diversity (arxiv:2605.12484) provides a plasticity benefit exceeding the distribution mismatch cost |

arxiv:2605.12484 (section 3.2) predicts outcome (c): domain diversity reduces entropy
collapse rate, preserving the model's ability to adapt to new MBPP task batches even
beyond 200 tasks. The +2 pp gain may be a lower bound of what mixed training can achieve.

**Novel dimensions vs 57-experiment queue**:
- Every experiment in the queue uses a single training data source (MBPP aug)
- No experiment interleaves HumanEval problems into GRPO training
- EXP-053 uses HumanEval as an *evaluation* probe; EXP-058 uses it as a *training*
  signal — orthogonal roles

**Implementation**: The `train_data_supplemental` + `train_data_mix_ratio` spec fields
require a ~15-line runner extension: load both JSONL files, shuffle and interleave at

### EXP-059: `exp_2026_06_08_002_length_normalised_reward_grpo_200tasks`

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Kind | `grpo_continual` |
| Priority | 7 |
| Base model | Qwen/Qwen2.5-Coder-1.5B-Instruct |
| Train tasks | 200 (MBPP aug, excl. eval20) |
| Rollouts | 4 |
| LR / LoRA r | 5e-6 / 16 |
| Eval | MBPP eval100 |
| Novel fields | `reward="binary_length_normalised"`, `length_normalise_base="log"`, `length_normalise_denominator="response_token_count"` |
| Wall-clock est. | ~58 min (no extra computation; token counts available from existing tokenizer output) |
| GPU | auto (1× A800 80GB) |

**Rationale**: arxiv:2503.04548 ("Length-Normalised Reward for GRPO in Code Generation",
March 2025) was tagged in the 2026-05-28 arxiv sweep as relevant but was never queued
in any of the 57 subsequent experiments. The oversight is attributable to the paper's
Moderate (★★) relevance rating — lower than the credit-assignment papers (★★★) that
already occupied the priority slots.

All 57 queued experiments use `reward: "binary"`: a flat +1.0 for test-pass, 0.0 for
fail. This creates a verbosity bias: there is no gradient signal distinguishing a
15-token correct solution from a 120-token correct solution. In MBPP problems, verbose
solutions often achieve correctness by explicitly handling test-case-specific edge cases
(inline if-else chains) rather than implementing the general algorithm. GRPO trained on
binary rewards will learn both styles equally, which may explain why the +2 pp gain is
stable but not growing — the policy is mixing generalizable code patterns with test-case
memory and the two contributions partially cancel in eval on unseen test variants.

Length normalisation addresses this by making reward proportional to brevity:
```
reward(passing, T tokens) = 1.0 / log(T + 1)
reward(failing, T tokens) = 0.0
```
At 200 tasks × 4 rollouts, approximately 47% of rollouts pass (based on the base model's
47/100 pass rate). Of those ~376 positive-reward rollouts per training run, length
normalisation creates a preference ordering that was previously absent. Failing rollouts
are unaffected (reward stays 0.0), so the 53% failing-rollout gradient is unchanged.

**Relation to EXP-009 (P-GRPO partial reward, demoted)**:
EXP-009 assigned fractional rewards for partial code correctness (e.g., 0.3 for
compilation-without-test-pass). arxiv:2605.02944 showed this hurt vs binary because
it weakened the gradient signal on the negative side (failures now receive 0.3 instead
of 0.0, reducing the advantage magnitude on failing rollouts). Length normalisation
does *not* assign non-zero rewards to failing rollouts — the mechanism is completely
different. EXP-009 changed the reward *threshold*; EXP-059 changes the reward
*magnitude for passing rollouts only*.

**Implementation**: After collecting rewards, divide each 1.0 by `log(token_count + 1)`,
where token_count is available from the tokenizer. ~3 lines in the reward computation
block of runner.py.
