## Top 3 Relevant Papers

### 1. `arxiv:2605.12484` — Learning, Fast and Slow: Towards LLMs That Adapt Continually
*May 2026*

**Most mechanistically relevant paper for our 400-task degradation pattern.**

Identifies **plasticity loss** as the central failure mode in continual parametric LLM
adaptation: when a model is trained via policy-gradient methods (PPO, GRPO, etc.) for many
gradient steps, its output entropy shrinks, its KL to the base policy rises, and it loses
the ability to absorb new tasks cleanly — even when those tasks are well within the base
model's capability distribution.

The paper's proposed solution is a **fast-slow weight decomposition**: slow weights (the
main model parameters, updated at a low learning rate) retain generalizable patterns, while
fast weights (lightweight adapters or context embeddings, updated at a high learning rate)
absorb task-specific information. After task-specific training, fast weights are discarded
or merged selectively, preventing the slow weights from accumulating task-specific drift.

**Direct implication for our history:**
- flat-400 GRPO starting from the base model → entropy shrinks over 400 tasks → -1pt
- flat-200 GRPO → entropy is still acceptable → +2pt
- The transition from +2pt to -1pt at the 200→400 task boundary is exactly the
  plasticity-loss onset predicted by this paper
- **Actionable implication (EXP-024)**: warm-starting from the *already-converged*
  200×4 best adapter for 100 new tasks is a primitive approximation of the fast-slow
  framework's "start from a stable slow-weight checkpoint and add a new fast-weight
  phase." If the warm-start prevents the entropy collapse seen in flat-400, the
  adapter-chain approach is the practical path to extending the task horizon safely.

The paper does NOT require new infrastructure for our v0 test: the existing
`--resume-from-adapter` mechanism in `train_small_model_grpo_local.py` is sufficient.

### 2. `arxiv:2506.22716` — BEST-Route: Adaptive LLM Routing with Test-Time Optimal Compute
*June 2025, ICML 2025 (main conference)*

Addresses a key limitation of prior LLM routers: they route a query to *one* model and
generate *one* response, which wastes the cost-quality trade-off when a small model with
multiple samples can match a single large-model response at lower total cost.

BEST-Route introduces a **multi-headed router** that jointly decides:
1. Which model to use (small vs. large)
2. How many responses to sample from that model (n ∈ {1, 2, 4, 8})

The key empirical finding: for 30-50% of queries where the router has intermediate
confidence, using the small model with n=4 and selecting the best response (via a
lightweight judge or majority vote) matches the large model's single-sample quality at
a fraction of the cost. The router achieves a +3-5% accuracy improvement at the same
average inference budget by reallocating compute from certain-large-model calls to
best-of-N small-model calls for uncertain queries.

**Why relevant today:** Our router at threshold=0.57 makes binary decisions (small or
large model, always n=1). BEST-Route suggests a two-step improvement path:
1. First: characterise the full threshold Pareto curve (0.45 → 0.57 → 0.65)
2. Then: for the uncertain-confidence band [0.55, 0.75], add best-of-2 from the small
   model rather than defaulting to the large model

EXP-025 (today's threshold=0.65 eval) is Step 1. A future experiment combining
threshold tuning + best-of-N for the uncertain band is Step 2 (not queued today due
to backlog; defer to when queue drops below 15).

### 3. `arxiv:2605.05262` — InfoTree: Maximizing Rollout Informativeness under a Fixed Budget
*May 2026*

Formalises **Rollout Informativeness under Fixed Budget (RIFB)** as the expected
non-vanishing policy-gradient mass that a rollout set injects into GRPO. A rollout
group is "informative" iff it contains at least one passing and one failing trajectory
(mixed outcome); all-pass and all-fail groups have zero RIFB. InfoTree uses a tree-search
strategy (UUCB + Adaptive Budget Allocator) to find prompts with high RIFB — essentially,
training data curation guided by gradient efficiency.

**Key alignment with our project history:**
- Our 200-task MBPP training set has ~20% all-pass tasks and ~53% all-fail tasks,
  leaving ~27% tasks with non-zero RIFB
- This means ~73% of training compute (4 rollouts × 73 tasks) produces zero gradient
  signal under standard GRPO
- InfoTree's RIFB formulation is the theoretical backing for DAPO dynamic sampling
  (already queued as EXP-013 / exp_2026_05_18_002), which empirically addresses
  the same problem without the tree-search overhead

**Why deferred (not queued today):**
- The tree-search component of InfoTree requires a significant runtime infrastructure
  change (UUCB exploration + ABA) that cannot be implemented within the existing
  `runner.py` `grpo_continual` kind in a 4h A800 session
- The simpler manifestation (discard uninformative rollout groups) is already covered
  by EXP-013 (DAPO); testing the full InfoTree tree-search is a medium-term experiment
  requiring runner v2 infrastructure (estimated: 1-2 days of implementation work)
- **Recommendation:** When EXP-013 (DAPO) returns results, if RIFB improvement is
  significant, promote the full InfoTree tree-search to the next major batch

### EXP-024 · `exp_2026_05_22_001_warmstart_continual_15b_chunks34` · Priority 7
**Kind:** `grpo_continual`
**Model:** Qwen2.5-Coder-1.5B-Instruct
**Spec:** warm_start_adapter=200×4 best adapter, chunks=[3,4], 1 epoch/chunk,
4 rollouts/prompt, lr=5e-6, LoRA r=16, qwen-chat, binary reward, eval@100

**Rationale:** arxiv:2605.12484 ("Learning, Fast and Slow", May 2026) identifies
plasticity loss — output entropy shrinkage and rising KL to the base policy — as the
mechanism behind degradation in extended GRPO runs. Our history:
- flat-200 (base → chunks 1+2): +2pts (entropy still acceptable)
- flat-400 (base → chunks 1+2+3+4): -1pt (plasticity loss onset)
- staircase-200 (EXP-016: [100+100]): in queue, tests 2-chunk sequential chaining
- staircase-300 (EXP-020: [100+100+100]): in queue, tests 3-chunk sequential chaining

This experiment fills a distinct gap: **warm-starting from the fully converged best
adapter** (not a mid-training staircase checkpoint) and continuing for 100+100 more
tasks. The distinction matters because:

1. The 200×4 best adapter has spent 200 tasks fully converging to a good policy
   distribution. It is in a lower-energy, more stable weight configuration than a
   staircase stage-2 checkpoint (which has only seen 100 tasks)
2. The "fast-slow" framework in 2605.12484 predicts that a more stable slow-weight
   initialization enables the fast-weight phase (next 100 tasks) to adapt without
   disrupting the slow weights' entropy, which is exactly what we are testing
3. The key comparison: warm-start+100 (this experiment) vs. staircase-300 (EXP-020)
   will reveal whether the degradation is attributable to the starting-point quality
   (converged adapter vs. transient checkpoint) or to the number of total tasks

**Runner change required:** Add `warm_start_adapter` field to `run_grpo_continual`;
set `prev = spec.get("warm_start_adapter")` before the chunk loop (2-line change).

**Estimated GPU time:** ~90 minutes (2 × 45-min chunks on A800)

### EXP-025 · `exp_2026_05_22_002_router_threshold_065_joint_cycle` · Priority 5
**Kind:** `joint_cycle_multiseed`
**Model:** Qwen2.5-Coder-1.5B-Instruct + BERT-base learned router
**Spec:** router_threshold=0.65, seeds=[42, 123], eval_limit=100, HumanEval

**Rationale:** arxiv:2506.22716 ("BEST-Route", ICML 2025) demonstrates that the routing
threshold defines a continuous Pareto curve between inference cost and answer quality, and
that characterising at least 3 operating points is necessary to determine: (a) where
accuracy saturates on the high-threshold end, and (b) how steeply accuracy degrades on the
low-threshold end. Currently our router has been characterised at:

| Threshold | Direction | Status |
|-----------|-----------|--------|
| 0.45 | Aggressive (more→small) | In queue (EXP-021) |
| 0.57 | Baseline (current) | Done (93.04% acc) |
| 0.65 | Conservative (more→large) | **This experiment** |

The 3-point sweep (0.45, 0.57, 0.65) gives us the shape of the Pareto curve:
- If accuracy at 0.65 is within 0.5pts of 0.57: plateau reached — the router can already
  handle the near-certain queries correctly at 0.57; routing more to large adds no quality
- If accuracy at 0.65 exceeds 0.57 by >2pts: there is a significant ceiling — the router
  at 0.57 is over-routing to the small model on borderline queries, and a threshold of 0.65
  or higher is needed for production deployment
- The 3-point characterisation also provides the data needed to parameterise BEST-Route's
  uncertainty band: if accuracy changes most steeply between 0.57 and 0.65, the uncertain
  band should span [0.57, 0.65], which is where best-of-N from the small model should be
  applied to replace the default large-model call

Using seeds=[42, 123] matches both EXP-005 (threshold=0.57) and EXP-021 (threshold=0.45)
for direct 3-way paired comparison on identical evaluation splits.

**Runner change required:** None — `router_threshold: 0.65` is a valid parameter variant
of the existing `joint_cycle_multiseed` kind.

**Estimated GPU time:** ~20 minutes (eval-only, no training)
