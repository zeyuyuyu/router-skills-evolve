## Top 3 Relevant Papers

### 1. TACO: When Implausible Tokens Get Reinforced — Tail-Aware Credit Calibration ★★★
**ArXiv ID:** 2607.07976 | July 9-11, 2026 | **Most recent paper today**

**Core finding:** In standard GRPO (and any uniform advantage assignment), all tokens in a winning rollout receive the same positive advantage A. "Tail tokens" — those with very low probability under the current policy (p_θ(t) << 1) — receive the same credit as decision-critical tokens. Because the policy converges by reinforcing high-probability outputs, low-probability tokens that happen to appear in correct solutions (unusual variable names, coincidentally correct loop bounds) get equal positive credit, contaminating the gradient direction. TACO estimates each token's tail-risk τ_i = softmax(−log p_θ(t_i)) and scales its credit: A_i = A · (1 − α · τ_i) with α=0.3. For losing rollouts, no suppression is applied (we want to push away from all tokens). Evaluated on Qwen2.5-7B, Llama-3.1-8B, Mistral-7B across 8 benchmarks: +1.3–2.8pp math, +0.8–1.5pp code (HumanEval, MBPP). Training stability improves (lower gradient variance, fewer loss spikes).

**Relevance to us:** ACR=52.4% means 43/82 GRPO groups are collapsed (zero gradient). The remaining 39 non-collapsed groups produce ALL our gradient signal. If these groups suffer from PCC — and there's no reason to think they don't — each of the ~1,400 non-collapsed training steps in a 4-cycle run is partially misdirected. TACO addresses this: it's a drop-in modification to the advantage broadcast in `grpo_train_simple.py` (~15 lines), requires no execution traces (only log-probs already computed during the forward pass), and is mathematically orthogonal to AVSPO (collapsed groups), sign advantage (all-fail formula), and RLCSD (contrastive distillation). Code's tail-token patterns differ from math: stylistic tokens (variable names) have low p_θ and low semantic importance → TACO suppresses them (good). Structural tokens (unusual but important constructs) have low p_θ but α=0.3 only partially suppresses → credit preserved (safe). Motivated **EXP-116**.

### 2. RL Forgets! Towards Continual Policy Optimization (CPO) ★★★
**ArXiv ID:** 2607.04364 | July 2026 | **Most recent paper — challenges prior assumptions**

**Core finding:** arxiv:2507.05386 (July 2025) showed RL fine-tuning (RFT/GRPO) "naturally" mitigates forgetting vs SFT, citing the on-policy anchor effect. The new paper (2607.04364) challenges this: on MRCL (7 diverse task types), RL does suffer from catastrophic forgetting when tasks diverge between rounds. The "natural mitigation" holds only within narrow, similar task distributions. Proposes CPO (Continual Policy Optimization): a replay-free continual RL framework adding a behavioral KL term between training cycles — L_CPO = L_GRPO + β · KL(π_{k-1}(·|x_prior) || π_θ(·|x_prior)) — where x_prior is sampled from a small frozen memory index (256 prompts via kernel herding, ~67 MB). Key properties: replay-free (no rollout buffer), parameter-agnostic (no Fisher matrix), LoRA-compatible (KL on outputs, not weights). On Qwen3-VL-8B: −13.7% forgetting, +7.0% pretrained capability vs. vanilla GRPO.

**Relevance to us:** Our 4-cycle GRPO pipeline is exactly the sequential continual RL setting CPO was designed for. Each cycle's task distribution shifts as the policy improves (easy tasks solved → harder tasks surface), creating the same divergence condition that triggers forgetting in CPO's experiments. The queued forgetting diagnostic (EXP-115, 0.6h) will quantify how much this affects us. If forgetting_rate ≥ 2%, CPO is the minimum-overhead remedy: LoRA-compatible, replay-free, only ~60 extra lines in `grpo_train_simple.py`. CPO is distinct from EWC (parameter-level, Fisher-based) and from replay (buffer-based) — it regularizes at the behavior level, which is aligned with our objective (preserve task pass rates across cycles). The natural measure-diagnose-fix chain is EXP-111 (method comparison) → EXP-115 (forgetting diagnostic) → EXP-117 (CPO remedy). Motivated **EXP-117**.

### 3. RePO: Replay-Enhanced Policy Optimization ★★ (Filed, not queued)
**ArXiv ID:** 2506.09340 | June 2025

**Core finding:** RePO extends GRPO with a replay buffer of historical rollouts: for each prompt, it mixes fresh on-policy samples with retrieved historical ones, increasing effective optimization steps by 48% at only 15% additional compute cost. Achieves +18.4 absolute points on math for Qwen2.5-Math-1.5B and +4.1pp for Qwen3-1.7B vs. GRPO. The replay buffer naturally breaks the zero-variance deadlock for tasks where the model has changed since the historical rollout (old rollout's rewards are now diverse vs. current policy's uniform rollouts). Also prevents continual learning forgetting by keeping prior-task rollouts in the training mix.

**Relevance to us:** RePO addresses both ACR=52.4% (off-policy rollouts with diverse rewards can break zero-variance groups when the model has evolved) and continual learning forgetting (replay buffer preserves old task behaviors). The +18.4pp math result is the strongest of any GRPO variant we've evaluated in the literature. However: (a) requires implementing a rollout buffer storage system (more invasive than CPO's KL term); (b) the math gains may not transfer to code generation (math has a longer exploration horizon; code has tighter binary execution feedback); (c) CPO is the superior mechanism if forgetting is the primary issue (replay-free, lower overhead). Filed for EXP-118 if CPO (EXP-117) and TACO (EXP-116) are insufficient to close the Full=Router gap.
