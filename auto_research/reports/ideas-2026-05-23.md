## Top 3 Relevant Papers

### 1. `arxiv:2603.16158` — Execution-Grounded Credit Assignment for GRPO in Code Generation
*March 2026 · Accepted: ICLR 2026 Workshop on Scaling Post-Training for LLMs (SPOT)*

**The highest-impact paper for our ceiling-breaking objective.**

EGCA identifies and fixes a fundamental coarseness in GRPO for code: the final binary
pass/fail reward is broadcast uniformly across ALL tokens of a generated program, even when
only a narrow local error caused the failure. A 200-line program that fails because of a
single off-by-one error in one line receives the same per-token gradient signal as a
completely wrong program.

**The fix**: for every rollout, execute both the generated code and the canonical reference
solution under identical instrumentation. Find the *earliest semantic divergence*: the first
point in the execution trace where outputs differ (wrong variable value, wrong branch taken,
wrong return type). Assign non-zero advantage only to the token span that PRECEDES this
divergence; mask all downstream tokens. Result: the model receives gradient signal precisely
on the tokens that made the decision that led to failure, rather than noisy gradients
across the full 192-token output.

**Why this matters for our project:**

- Our vanilla GRPO baseline: ~53/100 MBPP eval tasks consistently fail ALL 4 rollouts
  → zero advantage on these tasks → ~73% of gradient steps carry no useful signal
- Under EGCA: even a rollout that fails all test cases receives token-level advantage on
  the token span up to the first execution divergence from a correct trace — converting
  zero-gradient tasks to *positive-gradient* tasks
- Paper result on MBPP: **+1.5 pts pass@1** over vanilla GRPO. Applied to our baseline
  (47/100), this predicts 47→48.5→rounding→49 or 50 — at or above our current ceiling
- Paper result on HumanEval: **+3.1 pts** — significant and consistent across benchmarks
- **18% wall-clock overhead** only: no critic network, no auxiliary loss, no learned verifier
  needed — the canonical reference solutions are already present in the MBPP dataset

**Key mechanistic distinction from all 25 queued experiments:**

All queued experiments address the gradient-efficiency problem by changing *which tasks*
get gradient steps (DAPO: discard non-mixed groups), *how often* hard tasks appear
(MT-GRPO: upsample hard tasks), or *how much* they are weighted (F-GRPO: focal advantage).
EGCA changes what gradient signal a SINGLE FAILED ROLLOUT CARRIES — fundamentally
different and combinable with any of the above.

### 2. `arxiv:2603.09892` — MSSR: Memory-Aware Adaptive Replay for Continual LLM Fine-Tuning
*March 2026*

MSSR introduces an **Ebbinghaus forgetting-curve-inspired replay schedule** for continual
LLM fine-tuning. The core insight: the Ebbinghaus curve (which characterises human memory
decay as an exponential function of time since learning) has a direct analogue for neural
networks — samples that were recently learned (high reward, low loss after training) begin
forgetting faster than they decay later, and optimal rehearsal matches these dynamics.

**Method:** MSSR maintains a replay buffer of the N highest-reward examples seen during
training. Rather than replaying on a fixed schedule (e.g., every K steps), it monitors
each buffered sample's current loss. When current_loss > memory_threshold (a proxy for
"forgotten"), the sample is scheduled for replay. As the model stabilises and loss on
buffered examples decreases, replay intervals expand automatically — early training sees
frequent replay; later training sees sparse replay, saving compute.

**Results:** Across 3 backbone models and 11 sequential tasks, MSSR outperforms all
state-of-the-art replay baselines (EWC, L2P, O-LoRA). Strongest gains on
reasoning-intensive and code-adjacent tasks.

**Why relevant to our degradation problem:**

- Our history: flat-200 GRPO → +2pts; flat-400 GRPO → -1pt. The degradation between
  200 and 400 tasks is exactly the scenario MSSR is designed for: tasks 1-200 are
  "forgotten" while the model trains on tasks 201-400
- MSSR's replay would periodically revisit the highest-reward examples from tasks 1-100
  (the easy/learnable subset) during training on tasks 101-300, preventing the model from
  "unlearning" what it learned in early training
- This is mechanistically orthogonal to the three currently-queued 300-task approaches:
  KL penalty (EXP-002) regularises the RL objective; curriculum (EXP-011) reorders
  training; warm-start (EXP-024) changes the initialization. MSSR instead directly
  counteracts forgetting via explicit rehearsal
- The key comparison experiment: MSSR@300 (EXP-027) vs. KL@300 (EXP-002) — which
  anti-forgetting mechanism is more effective? If MSSR matches KL+curriculum without
  any distributional constraint, that would suggest replay is the right long-term path
  for continual GRPO

### 3. `arxiv:2605.13162` — Continual Fine-Tuning of Large Language Models via Program Memory
*May 2026*

**Why noted but not queued today:**

ProCL organizes LoRA adapters into "program memory slots" — a structured bank of adapters
where each slot specialises for a different task cluster. At inference, input-conditioned
attention selects the most relevant slot(s) and blends their weights. This prevents
cross-task interference and achieves strong retention on sequential code tasks, evaluated
on Qwen3-4B and Qwen3-8B.

Why not queued: ProCL requires a fundamentally different training infrastructure (managing
a bank of parallel LoRA adapters with a learned attention mechanism), which cannot be
implemented within the existing `runner.py` `grpo_continual` kind in a 4h session.
The method's target (Qwen3-4B, 8B) is also one generation ahead of our Qwen2.5 models.
**Recommendation:** Promote to active queue in the next batch AFTER the A800 is restored
and we have results from EXP-002 (KL@300) to determine whether we need more sophisticated
continual mechanisms.

### EXP-026 · `exp_2026_05_23_001_egca_execution_grounded_grpo_15b` · Priority 8
**Kind:** `grpo_continual`
**Model:** Qwen2.5-Coder-1.5B-Instruct
**Spec:** 200 tasks, 4 rollouts/prompt, 1 epoch, lr=5e-6, LoRA r=16, qwen-chat,
binary reward, `credit_assignment=execution_grounded`, `egca_reference_field=canonical_solution`, eval@100

**Rationale:** arxiv:2603.16158 (EGCA, ICLR 2026 Workshop SPOT) — execution-trace-based
token-level credit assignment for GRPO. Rather than broadcasting pass/fail reward uniformly
across all output tokens, EGCA identifies the first execution divergence between the
generated program and the reference solution, and assigns advantage only to the token span
preceding that divergence.

For our project's 53% consistently-failing MBPP tasks: under vanilla GRPO these are
zero-gradient groups. Under EGCA, even all-fail rollouts contribute positive gradient
signal at the token span preceding the first execution error — converting them from
noise to signal. Paper reports +1.5 pts on MBPP vs vanilla GRPO, which would bring us
from 47→48.5-49 (matching or exceeding our current ceiling from a different mechanism).

Critically orthogonal to all 25 queued experiments: F-GRPO/DAPO/MT-GRPO/Unlikeliness
all change *which tasks or rollouts receive gradient*; EGCA changes *what signal a single
failed rollout carries at the token level*. These are composable interventions — if EGCA
and F-GRPO are both run and both work, combining them is a natural follow-on.

**Runner change required:** Add `credit_assignment: "execution_grounded"` field to
`grpo_continual` kind; implement execution-trace diffing using the `canonical_solution`
field already present in MBPP augmented dataset. No extra data needed.

**Estimated GPU time:** ~110 minutes (90 min baseline × 1.18 EGCA overhead)

### EXP-027 · `exp_2026_05_23_002_mssr_replay_grpo_15b_300tasks` · Priority 7
**Kind:** `grpo_continual`
**Model:** Qwen2.5-Coder-1.5B-Instruct
**Spec:** 300 tasks, 4 rollouts/prompt, 1 epoch, lr=5e-6, LoRA r=16, qwen-chat,
binary reward, `replay_buffer_size=30`, `replay_freq_init=5`, `replay_freq_expand_rate=1.5`, eval@100

**Rationale:** arxiv:2603.09892 (MSSR, March 2026) — Ebbinghaus-inspired adaptive replay
for continual LLM fine-tuning. Maintains a buffer of the 30 highest-reward examples seen
during training; replay is triggered when current loss on buffered examples rises above a
threshold (proxy for forgetting). Initial replay frequency is 1 replay step per 5 gradient
steps, expanding to 1 per ~20 by the time 200 tasks are seen.

This directly attacks our key degradation problem (flat-200 → +2pts, flat-400 → -1pt) via
a mechanism not yet represented in the queue: explicit rehearsal. The three currently-queued
approaches to this problem all constrain the RL objective (KL penalty, EXP-002) or change
the training order (curriculum, EXP-011) or initialization (warm-start, EXP-024). MSSR
replay instead prevents forgetting by showing the model past successes during later training.

Key comparison to be completed when both run: MSSR@300 (this experiment) vs. KL@300
(EXP-002, priority 9). If MSSR achieves comparable anti-forgetting with lower KL overhead,
it becomes the preferred long-term mechanism for extending the training horizon.

**Runner change required:** Add `replay_buffer_size`, `replay_freq_init`,
`replay_freq_expand_rate` fields to `grpo_continual` kind; implement circular replay
buffer that stores high-reward (reward=1.0) training examples and interleaves them at
the specified frequency. Simple addition to the training loop, ~50 lines.

**Estimated GPU time:** ~125 minutes (300 tasks × ~0.42 min/task × 1.1 replay overhead)
