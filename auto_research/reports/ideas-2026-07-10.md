## Top 3 Relevant Papers

### 1. Group-Standard-Deviation Identity Unifies GRPO Family ★★★
**ArXiv ID:** 2607.00152 | July 2026 (most recent)

**Core finding:** GRPO, Dr. GRPO, and DAPO all compute an update proportional to within-group reward standard deviation. The authors prove:

```
gradient_magnitude = f(std(r_1, ..., r_G))
```

For binary (0/1) rewards, all-fail groups: std = 0 → gradient = 0, identically. All-pass groups: std = 0 → gradient = 0. This is not a bug but an algebraic identity shared across all three algorithm variants. The paper characterizes the full "GRPO family" as parameterized by a "normalization dial" that controls where learning is concentrated.

**Three-path taxonomy for fixes:**
1. **Change reward** to ensure std > 0 even in degenerate groups (partial credit, relative ranking)
2. **Change baseline** to not be per-group mean (global EMA, Sign advantage, REINFORCE++)
3. **Skip degenerate groups** and reallocate compute (AERO, selective GRPO)

Our ongoing experiments: EXP-108 (Sign advantage, path 2), EXP-104 (selective group, path 3). Today's EXP-110 implements path 1 (partial credit). All three paths are now covered.

**AAAI value:** This July 2026 paper is the sharpest theoretical anchor for §5 "mechanistic analysis." Citing it: "We identify 52.4% of our training groups as degenerate under the group-std identity (arxiv:2607.00152), and evaluate three orthogonal fixes: Sign advantage (path 2), partial-credit reward (path 1), and selective-group GRPO (path 3)."

### 2. On-Policy Representation Distillation (OPRD) ★★★
**ArXiv ID:** 2606.06021 | June 4, 2026 (revised June 20, 2026)

**Core finding:** Standard on-policy distillation (OPD) supervises the student at the output layer only (next-token KL). OPRD adds hidden-state alignment: for every K-th transformer layer, minimize MSE between student and teacher hidden states on the same on-policy rollouts. This supervision bypasses the LM head bottleneck — the teacher's internal representations of algorithmic structure (variable tracking, recursion patterns) are transferred directly rather than being compressed through the LM head.

**Results:** OPRD is Pareto-dominant over output-space OPD: better accuracy, same or lower training cost, lower GPU memory than output-space + token-KL combinations. Gains concentrated on reasoning-intensive tasks where the LM head compression is most lossy.

**Relevance:** Our skills arm uses token-level KL on GPT-5.5 traces. The 20.7pp gap (75.61% → 96.34%) is too large to close purely via output-space supervision given the 1.5B→GPT-5.5 capacity ratio. OPRD's hidden-state alignment with a local Qwen3.5-4B pseudo-teacher gives the 1.5B student direct access to richer intermediate representations. Critical implementation note: GPT-5.5 is API-only — Qwen3.5-4B on A800 serves as the local pseudo-teacher, contributing hidden states while GPT-5.5 traces provide output-level targets. Both forms of supervision train on the same rollout batch. Motivates **EXP-111**.

### 3. Adaptive Efficient Rollout Optimization (AERO) ★★
**ArXiv ID:** 2602.14338 | February 2026

**Core finding:** In standard GRPO on 1B–7B models, 35%+ of queries are "zero-advantage" (all rollouts score identically). AERO maintains a Bayesian posterior over per-prompt pass rates and uses it to detect zero-advantage groups BEFORE rollout — skipping the API call or generation cost for prompts predicted to be degenerate. Reallocates the saved rollout budget to "Goldilocks" prompts (moderate difficulty, high learning signal). +2.8% GSM8K accuracy with 40% fewer rollout API calls.

**Relevance:** Our 52.4% ACR (all-fail rate) means 43/82 prompts waste G=8 rollouts per cycle. AERO would predict these from pass-rate history (available in our SkillBook per-prompt stats) and reassign those rollout slots to mixed-signal prompts. This is path-3 of the group-std fix taxonomy (skip degenerate groups). Not proposed as a new experiment today (EXP-104 covers path-3 with a different mechanism), but motivates adding AERO-specific difficulty-adaptive sampling as a future variant once EXP-104 results are available.

### EXP-110: Partial-Credit Reward for Guaranteed Non-Zero Group Variance (HumanEval)
**ID:** `exp_2026_07_10_001_partial_credit_reward_grpo_group_std_identity_humaneval`
**Kind:** `grpo_continual` | **Priority:** 8 | **Estimated GPU:** 1.5h

**Motivation:** arxiv:2607.00152 (July 2026) proves that the gradient magnitude for all GRPO variants is proportional to within-group reward std. Binary rewards produce std=0 for all-fail groups (43/82 = 52.4%) → zero gradient by algebraic identity. This is path-1 of the three-fix taxonomy: change the reward structure to guarantee std > 0 in all groups. Partial-credit reward: `r = 0.05 * compile_ok + 0.10 * runs_without_exception + 0.85 * test_pass_fraction`. All-fail groups (none pass all tests) still differ in compilation and partial test pass rates → non-zero within-group std → non-zero gradient provably. Orthogonal to EXP-108 (Sign advantage, path 2) and EXP-104 (selective skip, path 3). If all three succeed, a combined EXP-112 can apply the most effective path or a composition of all three.

**Spec:** 1-cycle HumanEval, Qwen2.5-Coder-1.5B, G=8, SCALING_FORCE_BOTH=1. Modify reward function in `grpo_train_simple.py` to compute partial credit from compile check + execution check + per-assertion pass fraction. Three variants: (A) partial-credit + DAPO advantage, (B) partial-credit + Sign advantage (combines EXP-108), (C) binary reward + DAPO baseline. Track: fraction of groups with non-zero std (target: 100%), mean within-group std (target: > 0.1), full-test pass@1 (target: ≥ 93.68%), and compilation rate (sanity: ≥ 80%). ~25 lines change in reward function + ~20 lines in test harness.

**Target:** Full-test pass@1 ≥ 93.68% (+1pp). Fraction of groups with std > 0.001: drops from 47.6% (binary DAPO) to ~95%+. No reward-hacking: if full-solve rate per rollout decreases, increase w_pass from 0.85 to 0.95.

### EXP-111: OPRD Local Pseudo-Teacher Hidden-State Alignment for Skills Gap (HumanEval)
**ID:** `exp_2026_07_10_002_oprd_local_pseudo_teacher_hidden_state_align_skills_humaneval`
**Kind:** `joint_cycle_multiseed` | **Priority:** 7 | **Estimated GPU:** 2.0h

**Motivation:** arxiv:2606.06021 (June 2026). The 20.7pp skills gap (75.61% vs 96.34% large) is not a data problem — SCALING_FORCE_BOTH=1 gives GPT-5.5 traces on every task. OPRD shows output-space KL is bottlenecked by the LM head: the large model's hidden representations of algorithmic structure cannot be fully expressed in next-token distributions. Adding hidden-state alignment with a local Qwen3.5-4B pseudo-teacher bypasses this bottleneck. GPT-5.5 is API-only (no hidden states available), so Qwen3.5-4B serves as the hidden-state supervisor while GPT-5.5 traces remain the output-level supervision target. Both signals train on the same SFT batch.

**Spec:** 1-cycle HumanEval, skills arm only (no GRPO). In `train_small_model.py`: load Qwen3.5-4B in inference mode (~8 GB bf16), add learnable linear projections per aligned layer (d_student=2048 → d_teacher=3072 per layer). For each training batch: run teacher forward pass (no_grad, +15-20% wall time), compute L_hidden = MSE(projected student hidden states, teacher hidden states) at layers 4, 8, 12, 16. Total loss: L_CE + 0.1 * L_hidden. Three variants: (A) OPRD with Qwen3.5-4B teacher, (B) standard CE baseline (75.61%), (C) OPRD + DemoPSD Category-A filter (combines EXP-107). Peak GPU usage: ~11 GB for both models; well within A800 80 GB.

**Target:** Skills arm pass@1 ≥ 77.5% (+1.9pp from 75.61%). Hidden-state alignment loss converges within 200 SFT steps. Hard-task improvement > easy-task improvement (alignment most informative where LM head is most bottlenecked). Variant C (OPRD + DemoPSD) expected to outperform variant A (OPRD alone).
