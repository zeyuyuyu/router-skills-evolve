## Top 3 Relevant Papers

### 1. GRAIL: Gradient-Reweighted Advantages for RL with Verifiable Rewards ★★★
**ArXiv ID:** 2606.04889 | June 2026 | NTU DeCLaRe Lab | **Motivated EXP-118**

**Core finding:** GRPO's uniform advantage broadcast assigns equal gradient weight to all tokens, including uninformative filler (indentation, brackets, comments) and decision-bearing tokens (data structure choice, algorithm selection, boundary conditions). GRAIL introduces gradient-activation saliency — `s_i = ‖grad_{h_i} L‖ × ‖h_i‖` — as a per-token weight for the advantage. Tokens where perturbation to the last hidden state strongly affects the GRPO loss receive amplified advantage (A_i = A × (1 + γ × normalize(s_i)), γ=0.5); tokens with near-zero saliency receive reduced advantage. Implementation requires one extra backward pass w.r.t. hidden states (not parameters) per rollout batch, adding ~50% compute overhead. Results across Qwen3-1.7B, 4B, 8B, R1-distilled, and OctoThinker on math-reasoning benchmarks: **+3.60% average accuracy, +3.05% Pass@3** vs. vanilla GRPO. Outperforms token-entropy reweighting and step-level PRMs at lower implementation cost.

**Relevance to us:** Our 39 non-collapsed GRPO groups (ACR=47.6%) produce all gradient signal. Under uniform broadcast, ~70% of the token budget is spent on filler tokens. GRAIL redirects that budget to the ~30% of tokens that are algorithmically decision-bearing. Expected effect: 50% more effective gradient from the same rollouts, without increasing ACR or adding any distillation term. **Distinct from TACO (EXP-116)**: TACO uses token log-probability as the weighting signal (suppresses noise in winning rollouts only); GRAIL uses gradient-activation as the weighting signal (amplifies signal across all rollouts). Composable: TACO-GRAIL doubly targets credit quality. If both positive, EXP-120 (composition) is the natural ceiling experiment before AAAI deadline.

### 2. Denser ≠ Better: Limits of On-Policy Self-Distillation for Continual Post-Training ★★★
**ArXiv ID:** 2607.01763 | July 1, 2026 | Meng Wang et al., NLPR/MAIS | **Motivated EXP-119**

**Core finding:** On-policy self-distillation (SDPO) adds a dense KL-matching term to GRPO, believed to improve training signal density and stability. This paper shows the opposite in continual post-training: SDPO causes **stronger forgetting** than vanilla GRPO — larger parameter drift (‖ΔLoRA‖), larger response-space drift (KL from pre-training distribution), and self-reinforcing formatting artifact amplification through the teacher-student loop. Critically, GRPO is the CONSERVATIVE learner: its on-policy KL penalty implicitly limits parameter drift. Adding dense distillation terms removes this conservation and increases plasticity at the cost of forgetting.

**Relevance to us:** We have two queued experiments adding dense distillation to GRPO: EXP-112 (forward-KL teacher distillation from GPT-5.5) and EXP-114 (RLCSD contrastive self-distillation). If arxiv:2607.01763's finding generalizes to our single-task iterative code RL setting, adopting EXP-112/EXP-114 for the full 4-cycle pipeline would increase cross-cycle forgetting. EXP-119 is a fast (~0.5h) post-hoc eval that checks whether the parameter drift signal is already visible after 1 cycle of RLCSD, guiding whether to de-prioritize EXP-112/EXP-114 in the AAAI paper. If the finding doesn't generalize (likely — single-task, stable teacher, code), we get a novel negative result: "Denser ≠ Better is specific to multi-task settings; single-task code GRPO distillation is safe." Either outcome strengthens the paper.
