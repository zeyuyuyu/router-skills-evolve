## Top 3 Relevant Papers

### 1. "Improving Data Efficiency for LLM Reinforcement Fine-tuning Through Difficulty-targeted Online Data Selection and Rollout Replay" (★★★)
**ArXiv ID**: 2506.05316 | Sun et al. | Submitted June 2025, revised February 16, 2026

**Core finding**: Two orthogonal sources of compute waste in GRPO are identified and addressed:
1. **Task waste**: ~70% of training tasks in typical RLVR setups are informationally inert
   (all-fail → zero advantage; all-pass → zero advantage). Difficulty-targeted online data
   selection scores tasks by their current mixed-outcome fraction and over-samples tasks
   near p=0.5 while under-sampling extremes. Scoring is re-computed every 10 steps so it
   adapts as the policy improves.
2. **Rollout waste**: freshly generated rollouts are expensive (~4000ms forward pass per
   rollout on A800); only ~27% of them in our setup produce non-zero advantage. Rollout
   replay maintains a FIFO buffer of past rollouts per task and replays 50% of each
   mini-batch from the buffer with importance-weighted advantage re-computation
   (IS weight = π_current/π_old, clipped to [0.5, 2.0]).

**Results**: Combined, difficulty selection + rollout replay achieves the same MBPP pass@1
improvement as vanilla GRPO in **47% of the wall-clock time** on a 12B model for math
reasoning. The accuracy gain from gradient quality improvement (more steps on informative
tasks) slightly exceeds the baseline in several configurations.

**Relevance**: Our 200-task MBPP setup has ~53 all-fail (26.5%), ~20 all-pass (10%),
~27 mixed (13.5%) training tasks. The 73% of rollout generation compute spent on zero-
advantage groups is precisely the waste this paper targets. The rollout replay buffer has
zero overlap with any of the 49 queued experiments — it is the first experiment that reuses
past rollouts rather than regenerating fresh ones every step.

### 2. "Adaptive Rollout Allocation for Online Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards" (★★★)
**ArXiv ID**: 2602.01601 | February 2026

**Core finding**: Fixed G rollouts per task is sub-optimal because rollout informativeness
is governed by the Bernoulli variance p×(1−p). The optimal budget allocation assigns
G_t ∝ sqrt(p_t × (1 − p_t)) where p_t is the task's current success probability: tasks
near p=0.5 (maximum variance → maximum advantage estimation noise) benefit most from
additional rollouts, while tasks near p=0 or p=1 have near-zero variance and should
receive the minimum budget. Given a fixed total rollout budget B = N_tasks × G_default,
redistributing to mixed-outcome tasks provably reduces the policy gradient variance without
increasing compute.

**Results**: On code generation benchmarks with verifiable rewards, adaptive allocation
matches or exceeds fixed-G=8 accuracy (1.6× the baseline rollout count) while spending
the same compute as fixed-G=4, and achieves 12% lower advantage estimation variance.

**Relevance**: Our 27 mixed-outcome tasks currently receive G=4 rollouts each — the same
as the 53 informationally inert all-fail tasks. Under adaptive allocation with total
budget 800 (200×4), the mixed tasks would receive G≈6–8 rollouts (the budget freed from
easy/hard tasks), giving substantially tighter advantage estimates on the tasks that
actually move the gradient.

### 3. "Faster Synchronous On-Policy RL via Straggler-Aware Group Sizing" (★)
**ArXiv ID**: 2606.02218 | Submitted June 2026

**Core finding**: In distributed synchronous GRPO, a single unusually-long rollout
(straggler) stalls reward computation for the entire group. SAGC proposes a dynamic
group-size controller that adapts G online based on observed rollout timing variance.
In a distributed 8-GPU setup, SAGC reduces training wall-clock by 23% with no accuracy
loss.

**Relevance**: Primarily addresses multi-GPU distributed training stragglers. On our
single A800 setup there are no inter-worker stragglers. However, the core insight —
adapting G based on observed rollout variance — is related to EXP-051 (adaptive budget)
and confirms the "adaptive G" direction. No new experiment proposed from this paper;
EXP-051 already captures the idea for single-GPU setting via informativeness-based
allocation.

### EXP-050: `exp_2026_06_04_001_rollout_replay_difficulty_selection_15b`

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Kind | `grpo_continual` |
| Priority | 8 |
| Base model | Qwen/Qwen2.5-Coder-1.5B-Instruct |
| Train tasks | 200 (MBPP aug, excl. eval20) |
| Rollouts | 4 (nominal; replay makes effective ≈ 8 per task per epoch) |
| LR / LoRA r | 5e-6 / 16 |
| Eval | MBPP eval100 |
| Novel fields | `online_data_selection="difficulty_targeted"`, `selection_window=10`, `rollout_replay=True`, `replay_buffer_size=400`, `replay_fraction=0.5`, `is_clip_lo=0.5`, `is_clip_hi=2.0` |
| Wall-clock est. | ~80 min (50% fewer fresh rollouts) |
| GPU | auto (1× A800 80GB) |

**Rationale**: arxiv:2506.05316 (Sun et al.) identifies two orthogonal compute sinks in our
standard GRPO loop. First, ~73% of per-step rollout generation targets informationally inert
tasks (all-fail or all-pass groups with zero advantage); difficulty-targeted online selection
re-estimates each task's mixed-outcome probability every 10 steps and over-samples near-p=0.5
tasks dynamically. Second, fresh rollout generation costs ~4000ms per rollout on A800 even
for tasks where recent rollouts are still on-policy; replay_fraction=0.5 draws half of each
mini-batch from a per-task FIFO buffer (replay_buffer_size=400 ≈ 2 past rollout sets per
task), importance-weighted as π_current(rollout) / π_old(rollout) clipped to [0.5, 2.0].
The combined effect: every gradient step is computed from higher-informativeness rollouts
(difficulty selection) and twice as many effective samples per rollout generation call
(replay). Wall-clock drops to ~80 min.

**Distinction from all 49 queued experiments**: MT-GRPO (EXP-019) adjusts task sampling
frequency but regenerates fresh rollouts every step. DAPO (EXP-013) discards zero-advantage
groups and resamples fresh replacements. Curriculum-GRPO (EXP-001) uses a static pre-training
sort. **No existing experiment reuses past rollouts with importance-weighted re-evaluation.**
This is the only rollout-replay experiment in the queue.

### EXP-051: `exp_2026_06_04_002_adaptive_rollout_budget_15b`

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Kind | `grpo_continual` |
| Priority | 7 |
| Base model | Qwen/Qwen2.5-Coder-1.5B-Instruct |
| Train tasks | 200 (MBPP aug, excl. eval20) |
| Total rollouts | 800 (same as baseline 200×4; redistributed per task) |
| LR / LoRA r | 5e-6 / 16 |
| Eval | MBPP eval100 |
| Novel fields | `rollout_budget="adaptive"`, `rollout_budget_min=2`, `rollout_budget_max=8`, `rollout_budget_total=800`, `rollout_budget_warmup=10`, `rollout_budget_window=8` |
| Wall-clock est. | ~90 min (same total compute as baseline) |
| GPU | auto (1× A800 80GB) |

**Rationale**: arxiv:2602.01601 (Adaptive Rollout Allocation) proves that the optimal
rollout budget per task under a Bernoulli reward model is G_t ∝ sqrt(p_t × (1−p_t))
where p_t is the task's current success probability estimated from the last 8 rollouts.
Applied to our 200-task setup: all-fail tasks (p≈0) and all-pass tasks (p≈1) receive
G_min=2; mixed-outcome tasks (p≈0.5) receive up to G_max=8 rollouts — with total budget
fixed at 800 (identical to baseline 200×4). The ~27 mixed-outcome tasks would receive
≈6–8 rollouts each (capped at G_max=8), giving tighter advantage estimates precisely where
GRPO training derives all its useful gradient signal. The ~73 easy/hard tasks are assigned
G_min=2 (enough to track their p_t for budget re-allocation but not wasteful).

**Distinction from all 49 queued experiments**: Fixed G=8 (EXP-003) doubles total compute
(200×8=1600 rollouts). MT-GRPO (EXP-019) adjusts task sampling frequency, not rollouts
per visit. F-GRPO (EXP-018) scales the post-generation advantage, not the number of
rollouts used to estimate it. **No existing experiment varies G per task at fixed total
budget.** This is the only adaptive-budget rollout allocation experiment in the queue.
