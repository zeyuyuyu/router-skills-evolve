## Top 3 Relevant Papers

### 1. Procedural Memory Distillation ★★★
**ArXiv ID:** 2607.01480 | July 1, 2026 | Salesforce AI Research, Purdue

**Core finding:** RLVR (GRPO/SDPO) discards the richer procedural information in each rollout after a single gradient update. PMD organizes procedural memory at three levels: raw trajectories, self-reflected strategies, and higher-level behavioral patterns. A memory-conditioned self-teacher (the same policy, conditioned on accumulated memory) supervises the student on its own rollouts. Central principle: **co-evolution** — policy generates rollouts → memory updates → memory conditions next rollouts → policy updates.

**Results:**
- +7.9-13.6% on LIVECODEBENCH vs SDPO (Qwen3-8B, OLMo3-Instruct-7B)
- +3.8-5.5% on SCIKNOWEVAL (scientific reasoning)
- Memory is inference-free: absorbed into policy weights during training, not carried at test time

**Relevance to us:** Our SkillBook extracts a procedure post-cycle and injects it at SFT/inference time — but the procedure is **frozen during GRPO Phase 3b**. PMD reveals a missed feedback loop: passing GRPO rollouts encode the current best strategies but this information is wasted rather than fed back as a training scaffold. EXP-110 adapts PMD's memory-conditioned self-teacher to our grpo_continual kind: live procedure buffer populated from passing rollouts, prepended to subsequent batch prompts, plus a λ=0.05 auxiliary CE loss over passing tokens conditioned on the buffer. No external API calls; the policy itself is both student and teacher. Motivated **EXP-110**.

### 2. Rollout-Level Advantage-Prioritized Experience Replay for GRPO ★★★
**ArXiv ID:** 2606.04560 | June 3, 2026 | Seoul National University

**Core finding:** GRPO discards every rollout after one update even though generating rollouts (autoregressive decode) is far more expensive than additional backward passes. Naive replay destabilizes training because policies drift fast. Three safeguards make it safe:
1. **Rollout-level granularity** (not group-level) allows mixing fresh and replayed rollouts
2. **Age eviction** (tau_max=4 steps) bounds staleness
3. **Off-policy importance weights** clip(π_θ/π_old, 0.8, 1.2) correct for drift

**Results:** Same-time higher pass rate vs. standard GRPO by recycling high-advantage rollouts. In their setup, top-30% replay fraction achieves comparable accuracy with ~70% of rollout generation compute.

**Relevance to us:** Our ACR=52.4% means 43/82 groups produce |advantage|=0 rollouts that go to the bottom of any priority queue → effectively never replayed. The 39/82 non-collapsed groups' high-|A| rollouts — currently used once — are replayed 1.7x on average. Net: 47.6% of tasks × 1.7x reuse ≈ 81% efficiency of a zero-ACR GRPO run, from rollout savings alone. Implementation: ~80 lines in grpo_train_simple.py with a Python heapq. No new models, no new rollout generation, no formula changes. Orthogonal to EXP-108 (Sign advantage) and EXP-104 (selective-group mask). If both EXP-108 and EXP-111 show gains, EXP-112 = Sign + RAPG. Motivated **EXP-111**.

### 3. GRPO, Dr. GRPO, and DAPO Are Three Operations on One Number ★★ (Diagnostic)
**ArXiv ID:** 2607.00152 | June 30, 2026

**Core finding:** For right-or-wrong (binary) rewards, the size of the policy gradient update for a group is exactly proportional to σ_i (the group standard deviation):
```
GRPO:    advantages = (r - mean(r)) / σ_i      # normalizes by σ
Dr.GRPO: advantages = r - mean(r)              # ignores σ (flat weight)
DAPO:    advantages = (r - mean(r)) / σ_i  if σ_i > 0, else skip (drop σ=0 groups)
```
This **group-standard-deviation identity** unifies all three algorithms. For binary rewards, σ_i ∈ {0, 0.5} — it's 0 when all rollouts pass or all fail (ACR groups), and 0.5 otherwise.

**Relevance:** Theoretical grounding for our ACR=52.4% problem: DAPO's group filtering is equivalent to upweighting σ_i > 0 groups by 1/σ_i and zeroing out σ_i=0 groups. The Sign advantage (EXP-108) is equivalent to Dr.GRPO with σ_i=1 for all groups — it assigns equal weight to all groups with a bipolar signal. This paper provides the mathematical justification for why Sign advantage (EXP-108) and Selective-Group GRPO (EXP-104) are theoretically grounded. **Not a new experiment** — but should be added to §4 theoretical analysis in the AAAI paper.

### EXP-110: Procedural Memory Distillation during GRPO Training (HumanEval)
**ID:** `exp_2026_07_07_001_pmd_procedural_memory_grpo_skills_humaneval`
**Kind:** `grpo_continual` | **Priority:** 7 | **Estimated GPU:** 2.0h

**Motivation:** arxiv:2607.01480 (July 1, 2026). Our SkillBook injects a fixed procedure at SFT/inference time but doesn't update during GRPO training. PMD's co-evolution principle shows that letting the policy's own passing rollouts build a procedure buffer — and then conditioning subsequent rollout prompts and adding a self-teacher auxiliary loss on it — yields +7.9-13.6% on LIVECODEBENCH. Applying this within our grpo_continual Phase 3b creates a feedback loop that SkillBook currently lacks: successful GRPO strategies become a live training scaffold that reduces future group collapse.

**Spec:**
- 1-cycle HumanEval, Qwen2.5-Coder-1.5B, G=8, SCALING_FORCE_BOTH=1
- In `grpo_train_simple.py`:
  1. Maintain `procedure_buffer = {}` (task_prefix → hint_str, max 50 entries, LRU)
  2. Before each rollout batch: prepend `# Verified strategy:\n{buffer[task]}\n---\n\n` for tasks in buffer
  3. After reward eval: upsert first 128 tokens of passing rollout body into buffer
  4. End-of-epoch: compute L_aux = CE(π_θ(passing_tokens | proc_prompt + problem)); add λ=0.05 × L_aux
  5. Track: buffer fill rate, pass_rate_with_hint vs. without_hint, L_aux curve
- Variants: (A) prompt conditioning only (no auxiliary loss), (B) full PMD (prompt + aux loss), (C) DAPO baseline

**Target:** pass@1 ≥ 93.68% or skills arm ≥ 77%. Buffer fill rate > 0.6 by epoch 2.

### EXP-111: Rollout-Level Advantage-Prioritized Replay for GRPO (HumanEval)
**ID:** `exp_2026_07_07_002_rapg_rollout_advantage_prioritized_replay_grpo_humaneval`
**Kind:** `grpo_continual` | **Priority:** 6 | **Estimated GPU:** 1.75h

**Motivation:** arxiv:2606.04560 (June 3, 2026). Our zero-variance groups (ACR=52.4%) waste rollout generation compute while producing |advantage|=0 rollouts. Replay with advantage-magnitude prioritization concentrates reuse on the 47.6% non-collapsed groups' high-|A| rollouts. The paper shows this is safe with age eviction (tau_max=4) + importance weight correction. Our implementation: Python heapq as the priority buffer, τ=0.3 replay fraction, importance clip [0.8, 1.2]. Zero-|A| rollouts sit at the bottom of the heap and are never sampled; high-|A| rollouts are replayed ~1.7x within their staleness window.

**Spec:**
- 1-cycle HumanEval, Qwen2.5-Coder-1.5B, G=8, SCALING_FORCE_BOTH=1
- In `grpo_train_simple.py`:
  1. `replay_buffer` = max-heap of (-|advantage|, step, log_probs_old, tokens, prompt_ids); max 200 entries
  2. After advantage computation: heappush rollouts with |advantage|>0; evict entries with step < current−4
  3. Each batch: n_fresh = 0.7 × batch_size fresh rollouts + n_replay = 0.3 × batch_size from heap
  4. Replayed rollouts: per-token loss × clip(π_θ/π_old, 0.8, 1.2)
  5. Track: buffer size, advantage distribution (replayed vs. fresh), wall-clock efficiency
- Compare: DAPO baseline, EXP-104 (selective-group), EXP-108 (sign advantage)

**Target:** pass@1 ≥ 92.68% with fewer rollout generation calls OR pass@1 ≥ 93.5% (+0.8pp). Buffer size > 60 by step 50.
