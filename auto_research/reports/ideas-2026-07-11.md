## Top 3 Relevant Papers

### 1. RECRL: Requirement-Aware Curriculum Reinforcement Learning for Code Generation ★★★
**ArXiv ID:** 2605.00433 | May 1, 2026 | Accepted at ACL 2026

**Core finding:** Existing GRPO-for-code methods use uniform random task sampling, which presents hard tasks (pass_rate ≈ 0) before the model can solve them → all-fail rollout groups → zero gradient → wasted compute. RECRL uses execution correctness (pass_rate per task) as a principled difficulty proxy, then trains in a curriculum from easy tasks first to hard tasks last.

**Key results:** RECRL consistently outperforms 5 SOTA baselines across 25 combinations (5 LLMs × 5 benchmarks), achieving +1.23%–+5.62% Pass@1 improvement. Gains are largest on medium-difficulty tasks (0.3 < difficulty < 0.7) — the mixed-group regime where gradient quality matters most. Hard tasks (difficulty > 0.7) benefit modestly; the model only begins to improve these once the policy is warm enough.

**Relevance to us:** Our GRPO sampler uses uniform random ordering. Approximately 40% of HumanEval tasks have difficulty ≥ 0.67 under our cycle-0 small model. These tasks produce all-fail rollout groups and contribute to the ACR=52.4% problem. RECRL's staircase (easy→medium→hard across epochs 1→2→3) is predicted to reduce the fraction of zero-variance groups in epoch 1 and warm up the policy before hard tasks are introduced. Distinct from EXP-108 (sign advantage acts on the advantage formula after a zero-variance group appears; RECRL prevents it from appearing). Distinct from EXP-104 (selective-group filters zero-variance groups at sampling time; RECRL reorders across epochs). All three are composable. Motivated **EXP-110**.

### 2. Reinforcement Fine-Tuning Naturally Mitigates Forgetting in Continual Post-Training ★★★
**ArXiv ID:** 2507.05386 | July 7, 2025 (updated June 2026)

**Core finding:** Systematic comparison of SFT vs GRPO-based reinforcement fine-tuning (RFT) in 7-task continual post-training on Qwen2.5-VL-7B-Instruct. When learning new tasks continuously:
- **SFT** overwrites earlier learned patterns → 8–15% forgetting of cycle-0 task accuracy by cycle-3
- **RFT (GRPO)** naturally preserves earlier knowledge because reward maximization is task-agnostic and the model must maintain generalizable capabilities to maximize reward → <3% forgetting

**Relevance to us:** We have four arms across four cycles, but we have never formally measured forgetting. The skills arm (SFT only) is predicted to exhibit 8–15% forgetting of easy HumanEval tasks it solved in cycle 0. If this is confirmed by our forgetting_eval, it provides a mechanistic explanation for part of the 20.7pp skills gap: the skills arm not only struggles with hard tasks (Category-A problem, EXP-107) but also regresses on easy tasks it previously solved. This dual failure — hard-task ceiling + easy-task forgetting — would substantially strengthen the paper's narrative motivating GRPO. The forgetting_eval is eval-only (<30 min, <0.5 GPU-hours) and can be run as soon as A800 is restored. Motivated **EXP-111**.

### 3. SoftMoE: Soft Differentiable Routing for Mixture-of-Experts in LLMs ★★ (Reference)
**ArXiv ID:** 2606.17952 | June 16, 2026 | Accepted at ICML 2026

**Core finding:** Replaces discrete top-k routing (not differentiable) with a truncated soft topk LapSum relaxation, allowing gradient-based optimization of expert routing. Parameterizes mean active experts per layer; learned allocation is highly non-uniform (later layers use more experts). Performance ≥ sparse MoE with significantly fewer active experts.

**Relevance to us:** Less directly applicable — our router is a binary logistic classifier (route to small or large), not an MoE soft router. However, the differentiable routing formulation is conceptually adjacent to making our router end-to-end differentiable with the GRPO objective. Filed as future reference; not queued as an experiment yet (would require architectural change beyond A800 4h budget).

### EXP-110: RECRL Execution-Difficulty Curriculum for GRPO (HumanEval)
**ID:** `exp_2026_07_11_001_recrl_execution_difficulty_curriculum_grpo_humaneval`
**Kind:** `grpo_curriculum_continual` | **Priority:** 7 | **Estimated GPU:** 2.0h

**Motivation:** arxiv:2605.00433 (May 1, 2026). Uniform random GRPO sampling presents hard tasks (pass_rate ≈ 0) before the model can solve them, producing all-fail rollout groups → zero gradient → ACR=52.4%. RECRL uses execution pass_rate from traces.jsonl as a difficulty proxy (difficulty_i = 1 − pass_rate_i), then trains in a 3-bucket staircase: epoch-1 easy (difficulty < 0.33), epoch-2 easy+medium (difficulty < 0.67), epoch-3 all tasks. Prevents all-fail groups from dominating early training by deferring hard tasks until the model has warmed up on easier ones. Orthogonal to EXP-108 (sign advantage, a formula-level fix) and EXP-104 (selective-group, a sampling-level filter) — composable with both.

**Spec:** 1-cycle HumanEval, Qwen2.5-Coder-1.5B-Instruct, G=8, DAPO advantage (no sign-advantage change), SCALING_FORCE_BOTH=1. Add curriculum DataLoader in `grpo_train_simple.py` (~20 lines): compute difficulty per task from cycle-0 traces, define buckets, filter per epoch. Compare: (A) RECRL staircase, (B) DAPO uniform-random baseline (reproduce 92.68%), (C) hard-first ordering (adversarial control). Track fraction of zero-variance groups per epoch, per-difficulty-bucket pass@1 improvement.

**Target:** pass@1 ≥ 93.5% (+0.8pp from 92.68%); fraction of zero-variance groups in epoch-1 ≤ 15% (vs 52.4% baseline). Medium-difficulty task improvement ≥ +2pp consistent with paper's largest-gain regime.
