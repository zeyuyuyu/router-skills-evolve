## Top 3 Relevant Papers

### 1. `arxiv:2503.06639` — GRPO's Effective Loss, Dynamics, and Success Amplification
*Guo et al., March 2025 (updated 2026)*

Central theoretical reference for this project. Key contributions:
(1) Proves the KL penalty is equivalent to reverse-KL from new policy to reference —
    removing it (as DAPO does) helps reasoning but risks collapse on small models.
(2) Identifies "success amplification collapse": when the model already solves most
    rollouts for a given task, advantage variance → 0 → zero gradient → only noise
    updates, which explains 400-task degradation.
(3) Shows gradient norm scales as lr × |advantage| — for 1.5B with noisy advantages,
    lr=5e-6 may sit close to the instability boundary. lr=1e-6 reduces per-step
    perturbation without requiring structural changes.

**Why relevant today:** Motivates both the conservative-LR experiment
(exp_2026_05_17_002) and directly supports the KL+curriculum combination
(exp_2026_05_17_001) by explaining why KL addresses a different failure mode than
curriculum does.

---

### 2. `arxiv:2106.09685` — LoRA: Low-Rank Adaptation of Large Language Models
*Hu et al., 2021*

Original LoRA paper. Key empirical finding: gain from rank increase is
significant from r=4→r=32 and saturates above r=64 for complex tasks.
The saturation threshold is task-dependent: translation saturates early
(r=4 is enough); code generation and reasoning tasks benefit from higher ranks
because the adaptation needs to modify deeper structural biases in token generation.

**Why relevant today:** Our history shows r=8 → r=16 improved the result (+1pt uplift
implied across model scale). The monotonic trend has not been tested at r=32 for the
1.5B GRPO case. Since GRPO requires the policy to learn structurally different
generation patterns (not just copying reference code), higher rank is theoretically
motivated for this training paradigm.

---

### 3. `arxiv:2601.19897` — Self-Distillation Enables Continual Learning Without Forgetting
*Jan 2026*

Demonstrates that models trained incrementally on subsets and then distilled from
their own earlier checkpoints prevent catastrophic forgetting better than any
one-shot training strategy. But the paper also provides the diagnostic finding that
motivated today's exp_2026_05_17_004: models that degrade on the training-domain
benchmark often *do not* degrade on held-out benchmarks at the same rate. The
authors call this "targeted forgetting" and distinguish it from general forgetting
by evaluating on multiple benchmarks before drawing conclusions. Applied to our
context: the 3B GRPO adapter degraded on MBPP (62→60) but we have no HumanEval
baseline to determine whether the degradation is targeted (MBPP distribution) or
general. This diagnostic evaluation must precede further 3B training proposals.

### EXP-010 · `exp_2026_05_17_001_kl_curriculum_15b_300tasks` · Priority 9
**Kind:** `grpo_curriculum_continual`
**Model:** Qwen2.5-Coder-1.5B-Instruct
**Spec:** 300 MBPP tasks, easy-to-hard curriculum (base-model solve rate), kl_coeff=0.02
against frozen reference, 4 rollouts/prompt, 1 epoch, lr=5e-6, LoRA r=16, qwen-chat,
binary reward, eval@100

**Rationale:** The two strongest pending interventions — KL regularization
(arxiv:2503.06639) and easy-to-hard curriculum (arxiv:2605.00433) — are being tested
in isolation in exp_2026_05_15_002 and exp_2026_05_15_001 respectively. They address
*orthogonal* failure modes: KL prevents distribution collapse during longer training
runs, while curriculum prevents zero-gradient rollouts on hard tasks seen too early.
Combining both on 300 tasks tests whether the gains are additive: KL stabilises the
extra 100 tasks beyond the safe 200-task ceiling, while curriculum ensures those 100
additional tasks are ordered so the model can learn from them. If each independently
gives +2–3pts, the combination on 300 tasks may reach +4–6pts — the target for a
paper-quality LLM training result. The no-KL no-curriculum baseline at 300 tasks has
not been explicitly run (only 200 and 400 tasks were tested without these fixes), so
this experiment also implicitly extends the task-count ablation to 300 with both
regularisers active. This is the **highest-priority** 2026-05-17 experiment.

**Estimated GPU time:** ~130 minutes (300 tasks + reference model doubles peak VRAM;
comfortably within 80GB for 1.5B + LoRA r=16).

### EXP-011 · `exp_2026_05_17_002_grpo_15b_lr1e6_200x4` · Priority 7
**Kind:** `grpo_continual`
**Model:** Qwen2.5-Coder-1.5B-Instruct
**Spec:** 200 MBPP tasks, lr=1e-6, 4 rollouts/prompt, 1 epoch, LoRA r=16, qwen-chat,
binary reward, eval@100

**Rationale:** Our best case uses lr=5e-6. GRPO dynamics (arxiv:2503.06639) shows
effective gradient magnitude scales as lr × |advantage|. For the 1.5B model with
4-rollout advantages, the per-step perturbation at lr=5e-6 is near the stability
boundary implied by the degradation at 400 tasks. A 5× reduction to lr=1e-6 tests
whether the +2pt gain can be achieved with a more conservative, stable update that:
(a) reduces variance across seeds (validation of robustness), (b) enables longer
future runs (if lr=1e-6 is stable at 200 tasks, it may be safe at 300–400 tasks
without needing KL penalty). If lr=1e-6 matches +2pts at lower variance, it becomes
the new safe default. If it underperforms, lr=5e-6 is confirmed as necessary for the
model to escape local minima. Clean ablation: all other params identical to best case.

**Estimated GPU time:** ~90 minutes (same as baseline; LR change adds no overhead).

### EXP-012 · `exp_2026_05_17_003_grpo_15b_lora_r32_200x4` · Priority 6
**Kind:** `grpo_continual`
**Model:** Qwen2.5-Coder-1.5B-Instruct
**Spec:** 200 MBPP tasks, lr=5e-6, LoRA r=32, 4 rollouts/prompt, 1 epoch, qwen-chat,
binary reward, eval@100

**Rationale:** History shows a monotonic trend in LoRA rank: r=8 gives the 0.5B
baseline gain; r=16 gives the best 1.5B result (+2pts). The LoRA paper
(arxiv:2106.09685) demonstrates that for complex adaptation tasks (code generation
is classified as complex) the rank-to-performance relationship is significant through
r=32 and saturates above r=64. For GRPO specifically, higher LoRA rank means the
policy gradient can express richer weight-space updates — important when the target
behaviour requires structural changes to generation (test-passing code) rather than
surface-level token biases. With r=32 on 1.5B the adapter adds ~52M trainable
parameters (vs ~26M for r=16), still safely within 80GB with 4 rollouts. All other
params match the best case. If r=32 improves over r=16 (+3pts vs +2pts), rank becomes
a first-order lever alongside task count and curriculum.

**Estimated GPU time:** ~95 minutes (~5% slower than r=16 due to larger adapter in
forward/backward pass; generation cost unchanged).
