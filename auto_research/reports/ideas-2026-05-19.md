## Top 3 Relevant Papers

### 1. `arxiv:2506.02355` — Rewarding the Unlikely: Lifting GRPO Beyond Distribution Sharpening
*He, Fried, Welleck — June 2025*

Identifies a **degenerate rank bias** in GRPO: the group-relative advantage normalisation
systematically amplifies trajectories that are already probable under the current policy, while
neglecting rare-but-correct trajectories. The intuition: when 3 of 4 rollouts fail and 1
succeeds, the 1 correct rollout receives the same advantage magnitude as in a group where 3
of 4 succeed — but because it is the lone correct rollout it has lower marginal log-probability,
meaning any gradient update will reinforce the 3 failing modes more than the 1 successful mode.
GRPO therefore exhibits distribution *sharpening* — it makes the model more confident about what
it already does rather than exploring what it could do.

The paper proposes an **unlikeliness reward**: an auxiliary term added to the reward of correct
trajectories that is proportional to their rank-based rarity within the group. Rare correct
solutions receive `alpha × (1 − prob_rank)` additional reward. With `alpha=0.3, beta=1.0`,
this substantially improves pass@N across all N values, especially large N, and increases sample
diversity.

**Why relevant today:** This is a trajectory-level fix to the same bias that F-GRPO addresses
at the group level. F-GRPO (EXP-014, queued 2026-05-18) scales group advantage by inverse task
success rate. Unlikeliness reward explicitly amplifies rare correct trajectories *within* each
group. The two mechanisms are orthogonal — one adjusts inter-group weighting, the other
adjusts intra-group trajectory weighting. For our mixed-outcome tasks (27/100 tasks with 1
correct rollout out of 4), the unlikeliness reward directly amplifies that single correct
rollout's contribution. This is the highest-priority 2026-05-19 experiment.

### 2. `arxiv:2602.05547` — Multi-Task GRPO: Reliable LLM Reasoning Across Tasks
*February 2026*

Real-world GRPO deployments train on heterogeneous task distributions where some tasks
dominate gradient updates (high reward variance) while others stagnate (zero variance —
either trivially easy or permanently hard). Standard GRPO treats all tasks with equal
sampling probability, so the actual gradient signal is dominated by the variance-rich subset.

MT-GRPO introduces two components: (1) a **min-max task weight** that dynamically adapts
per-task sampling frequencies every `minmax_update_freq` gradient steps — tasks below the
average rolling success rate have their weight increased; (2) a **ratio-preserving sampler**
that guarantees the adapted weights are faithfully reflected in mini-batch composition rather
than being diluted by fixed-size batch constraints. In 3-task and 9-task settings MT-GRPO
achieves 16–28% absolute improvement on worst-task accuracy over standard GRPO (6% over DAPO),
while matching average accuracy.

**Why relevant today:** Our 53/100 consistently-failed MBPP tasks are the "worst tasks" in our
200-task training distribution — they receive zero gradient signal under standard GRPO because
all 4 rollouts fail. MT-GRPO's min-max oversampling would expose the model to those 53 tasks
more frequently, increasing the chance of observing at least 1 correct rollout per group and
providing a non-zero gradient. This is mechanistically distinct from all 9 grpo_continual
experiments currently in the queue.

### 3. `arxiv:2605.07112` — Switchcraft: AI Model Router for Agentic Tool Calling
*Agarwal — May 2026*

The first LLM router specifically optimized for **agentic tool calling** workloads. Switchcraft
trains a DistilBERT-based classifier on 5 function-calling benchmarks to predict whether a
given query can be handled correctly by a small model (cost-efficient) or requires a large
model (high accuracy). Key finding: the routing threshold is not a free parameter — it
defines a Pareto frontier between average accuracy and per-query cost. Switchcraft achieves
82.9% accuracy matching the best individual model while cutting inference cost by 84%.

**Why relevant today:** Our BERT-base router at threshold=0.57 achieves 93.04% routing
accuracy. However, the threshold has been evaluated only at one operating point. Switchcraft's
Pareto analysis motivates testing a lower threshold (0.45) that routes more queries to the
small model — if accuracy stays within 2-3pts of 93.04%, the cost savings in deployment are
substantial. This single data point below the current threshold completes the Pareto picture.

### EXP-018 · `exp_2026_05_19_001_unlikeliness_reward_15b` · Priority 9
**Kind:** `grpo_continual`
**Model:** Qwen2.5-Coder-1.5B-Instruct
**Spec:** 200 MBPP tasks, 4 rollouts/prompt, 1 epoch, lr=5e-6, LoRA r=16, qwen-chat,
binary reward, `unlikeliness_reward=True`, `unlikeliness_alpha=0.3`, `unlikeliness_beta=1.0`,
eval@100

**Rationale:** The rank bias in GRPO (arxiv:2506.02355) causes the 1-in-4 correct rollouts
in our 27 mixed-outcome tasks to be under-amplified relative to the 3 failing rollouts,
because the failing rollouts are more probable under the current policy (the model fails more
often than it succeeds). Standard advantage normalisation preserves the magnitude imbalance.
The unlikeliness reward adds `alpha * (1 - prob_rank)` to correct trajectories: the
1 correct rollout in a 1/4 group has rank=4 (least probable correct trajectory in the group),
receiving `0.3 * (1 - 0.0) = 0.3` extra reward — a 30% boost on top of the base binary
reward. This directly converts the rarity penalty into a rarity bonus. Unlike F-GRPO (which
multiplies the group-level advantage by `(1 - success_rate)^gamma`), unlikeliness reward
operates at the trajectory level, amplifying the specific rare-correct trajectories within
each group. The mechanisms are orthogonal: F-GRPO amplifies hard groups, unlikeliness reward
amplifies rare trajectories within those groups. A future combination experiment can test
both simultaneously.

**Estimated GPU time:** ~90 minutes
**Runner changes required:** Add `unlikeliness_reward`, `unlikeliness_alpha`, and
`unlikeliness_beta` fields to `grpo_continual` kind; implement trajectory-rank-based reward
augmentation after rollout collection and before advantage computation.

### EXP-019 · `exp_2026_05_19_002_mt_grpo_minmax_task_weight_15b` · Priority 8
**Kind:** `grpo_continual`
**Model:** Qwen2.5-Coder-1.5B-Instruct
**Spec:** 200 MBPP tasks, 4 rollouts/prompt, 1 epoch, lr=5e-6, LoRA r=16, qwen-chat,
binary reward, `task_weighting="minmax"`, `minmax_update_freq=10`, eval@100

**Rationale:** MT-GRPO (arxiv:2602.05547) addresses imbalanced task-level gradient
contributions — the same failure mode we observe with 53/100 consistently-failed MBPP tasks
that are never sampled with non-zero advantage under standard GRPO. The min-max task weight
algorithm tracks rolling success rates per task and, every 10 gradient steps, increases
the sampling weight for below-average tasks and decreases it for above-average ones. The
ratio-preserving sampler then over-samples the hard 53 tasks in subsequent mini-batches,
giving the model more exposure to the hardest problems per epoch. Key distinction: MT-GRPO
changes *which tasks* appear in a mini-batch (sampling), while F-GRPO changes *how much
gradient weight* existing groups receive (loss weighting). The two mechanisms are
complementary. MT-GRPO alone achieved 16-28% absolute worst-task improvement over standard
GRPO in multi-task settings; for our single-dataset MBPP case, the 53/100 all-fail tasks
are the "worst task" cluster. If the MT-GRPO oversampling increases the number of training
steps where those tasks produce a correct rollout from ~0 to even 1-2 per 50 steps, the
overall MBPP gain should exceed the +2pts ceiling. All other params match the best case.

**Estimated GPU time:** ~95 minutes
**Runner changes required:** Add `task_weighting` and `minmax_update_freq` fields to
`grpo_continual` kind; implement per-task rolling success rate tracker and weighted mini-batch
sampler with ratio-preserving property.

### EXP-020 · `exp_2026_05_19_003_staircase_3stage_15b_100x3` · Priority 6
**Kind:** `grpo_multi_seed_staircase`
**Model:** Qwen2.5-Coder-1.5B-Instruct
**Spec:** 3 staircase stages [offset=0,limit=100], [offset=100,limit=100], [offset=200,
limit=100], 1 epoch/stage, 4 rollouts/prompt, lr=5e-6, LoRA r=16, qwen-chat, binary reward,
seeds=[42], eval@100

**Rationale:** The 2-stage staircase [100+100] (exp_2026_05_16_004) tests the Self-Distillation
continual learning hypothesis (arxiv:2601.19897): incremental chunk training from checkpoints
prevents forgetting better than training on all tasks simultaneously. If the 2-stage result is
positive (≥ flat-200 at +2pts), the 3-stage [100+100+100]=300 tasks is the natural extension.
Our history shows flat-400 degrades (-1pt) but the 300-task regime has only been explored via
flat GRPO + KL (exp_2026_05_15_002) and KL+curriculum (exp_2026_05_17_001), not via staircase
chunking. The staircase mechanism and KL regularisation target the same outcome (stable
300-task training) via different mechanisms — staircase enforces structural continuity between
task chunks while KL enforces probabilistic proximity to the reference policy. Testing both
independently allows us to compare their effectiveness: if 3-stage staircase (EXP-020) matches
or exceeds KL-300 (EXP-015), structural continuity is sufficient and KL adds no value;
if KL-300 > staircase, the forgetting is probabilistic rather than structural. Each 100-task
stage runs ~45 minutes; 3 stages total ~135 minutes on one A800.

**Estimated GPU time:** ~135 minutes
**Runner changes required:** `grpo_multi_seed_staircase` kind already supports
`staircase_stages` as a list of dicts — extending from 2 to 3 stages requires no code change,
just the additional stage dict in the spec.
