## Top 3 Relevant Papers

### 1. Gradient Starvation in Binary-Reward GRPO ★★★
**ArXiv ID:** 2605.07689 | May 8, 2026

**Core finding:** Group-mean centering in GRPO produces zero advantage in two degenerate cases:
- All rollouts in a group fail (r=0 for all) → mean=0 → advantage=0 → zero gradient
- All rollouts succeed (r=1 for all) → mean=1 → advantage=0 → zero gradient (wasted compute, but rarer)

The authors prove the degeneracy rate always exceeds the naive Bernoulli prediction by Jensen's inequality. Empirically: 0.69 degeneracy rate at G=4 in logged Qwen3.5-9B GSM8K training.

**The fix — Sign advantage:**
```
A = 2r - 1    # r=1 → A=+1; r=0 → A=-1
```
All-fail groups: A=-1 for every rollout → gradient that pushes down the probability of each wrong token (failure descent). All-pass groups: A=+1 → gradient pushes up (reinforcement). No group ever produces zero gradient. No reference model needed.

**Results:** Sign advantage reaches 73.8% GSM8K accuracy vs 28.4% for normalized group-mean DrGRPO at G=4 (45.4 point gap, 7 seeds, p<0.0001).

**Relevance to us:** Our ACR=52.4% means 43/82 groups (all-fail) currently produce zero gradient under DAPO. Sign advantage converts each of these 43 groups into negative reinforcement: the model learns to push down wrong-token probabilities without needing π_ref logits (unlike SRPO/EXP-106). The implementation is 2 lines in `grpo_train_simple.py`: replace the group-mean-centered advantage tensor with `2 * rewards - 1`. SRPO (EXP-106) and Sign advantage attack the same ACR problem via different mechanisms — SRPO routes to SDPO, Sign changes the advantage formula — and could in principle be combined. Motivated **EXP-108**.

### 2. Multi-Rollout On-Policy Distillation via Peer Successes and Failures (MOPD) ★★★
**ArXiv ID:** 2605.12652 | May 12, 2026 (v2 June 1, 2026) | CMU, Microsoft, Purdue

**Core finding:** Standard on-policy distillation (OPD) distills each rollout independently, ignoring the other G-1 rollouts generated for the same prompt. MOPD conditions each rollout's teacher signal on *peer rollout context*: successes provide positive evidence for valid reasoning patterns, failures provide structured negative evidence about plausible mistakes to avoid.

Two peer-context constructions studied:
1. **Positive peer imitation**: prepend one successful peer rollout as a demonstration; teacher imitates patterns it shared with the peer success.
2. **Contrastive success-failure conditioning**: prepend one success and one failure; teacher conditions on the contrast, emphasizing what the success did differently.

**Results:** MOPD with contrastive conditioning improves on-policy distillation by 2-4% across diverse reasoning benchmarks vs. standard OPD, with larger gains on hard tasks.

**Relevance to us:** Our SFT pipeline (Phase 3a) distills each (query, large_trace) pair independently. With G=8 rollouts per query, we already have rich peer context but ignore it. MOPD implementation in `traces_to_sft.py`: for each large-model trace selected for SFT, augment the training prompt by prepending one peer small-model success (from the same query's rollouts, if any) as positive context, and one peer failure as negative contrast. The small model learns not just from the large-model trace but from the contrast of what it already does right vs. wrong on the same query. Complementary to DemoPSD (EXP-107): DemoPSD addresses WHAT to distill (Category A disagreement traces), MOPD addresses HOW to distill (peer context). Could be combined in a future experiment. Motivated **EXP-109**.

### 3. On the Geometry of On-Policy Distillation ★★ (Diagnostic)
**ArXiv ID:** 2606.07082 | June 5, 2026 (v3 June 14, 2026)

**Core finding:** OPD updates rapidly lock into a narrow low-dimensional subspace ("subspace locking"). Constraining training to this early-formed subspace preserves OPD performance but substantially degrades SFT, showing the locked subspace is functionally sufficient for OPD. OPD sits in a "relaxed off-principal regime": fewer weights affected than SFT, less constrained than RL.

**Relevance:** Explains why our skills arm SFT (which uses OPD-like supervision from large traces) tends to generalize to easy tasks but struggles on hard ones. The subspace locking phenomenon implies that SFT on hard Category-A tasks (DemoPSD, EXP-107) benefits from early hard-task exposure before the OPD subspace locks. This supports the DemoPSD experiment's design and informs when in training to apply the disagreement filter. **Not a new experiment** — diagnostic insight that supports EXP-107.

If ≥1 rollout passed: use one passing rollout as "peer_success" context (prepend as `# Example of what you can do:\n{peer_success}`). If ≥1 rollout failed: use one failing rollout as "peer_failure" context (prepend as `# Example of a mistake to avoid:\n{peer_failure}`). Format: `{peer_success}\n{peer_failure}\n\n---\n\n{problem}`. Maximum peer context: 512 tokens (truncate if longer). Three variants: (A) positive-only peer (success context), (B) contrastive (success + failure), (C) no peer context (baseline 75.61%). All share the same rollout data from SCALING_FORCE_BOTH=1 collection. ~30 lines in traces_to_sft.py.

**Target:** Skills arm pass@1 ≥ 77.5% (+1.9pp from 75.61%) in best variant. Hard-task (Category A) accuracy improves more than easy-task (Category B). Peer context should increase effective information per training example without requiring new rollouts. Contrastive conditioning (variant B) expected to outperform positive-only (variant A) per MOPD paper.
